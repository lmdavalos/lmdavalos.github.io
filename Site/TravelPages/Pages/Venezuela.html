If history is destiny, geography is chance. Venezuela is to Colombia both destiny and chance, la hermana república whose sisterly tantrums involve generals claiming this or that territory or, more recently, the offer of mediating in our own confusing wars. Before Fabián invited us to Venezuela I had already worked out in my mind how, unable to show Alice and Keith what Colombia was like, Venezuela would be the place to go. So by the time the invitation was actually in place I already had reservations and connecting flights to, of course, Colombia. So maybe I expected some kind of watered down Colombia, a place where people look the same but speak funny and there is no ever-present war.


We reached Caracas in the middle of the night. Immigration was smooth, I was asked if I had any relatives in the country, said no and walked on. Later Fabián would tell me while zooming through the empty throughways of Caracas, that five million Colombians grace our neighbor. If this is indeed true then my family has actively avoided Venezuela in favor of New Jersey or the Flanders (really, what are these people thinking!). We headed west and then slightly south on what would become our favorite highway. Since our car had no cd player we decided to enjoy the local tunes and quickly discovered the horrible truth: Colombia exports "chucu chucu," tacky music for the tragically hick with a latin beat, to Venezuela. And Venezuelans must love it; every radio station was playing some form of it. Thus began the most ardently argued question of the trip: is Pastor López Colombian or Venezuelan?1


We reached "La Llanada," a summer camp on the northern edge of the Llanos in Portuguesa, on time to meet Fabián's truly extended family. We spent our first day there hiking up a stream with five waterfalls, two of them holding small caves with bats, vampire bats! Most of our blood, however, went to the relentless clouds of mosquitoes that could easily compete with those in the Cockpit Country. And yet the most exciting moment of the 30th of December was still to come, yes that evening we witnessed a surreal Santa on horseback, Papá Noel Llanero, bring presents to the numerous children on board. More surreal even, I went on horseback for the first time in almost a decade of dreading horses (this after my last equestrian experience: near decapitation by clotheslines back in Colombia). New Year's eve had to top all THAT, and it did: we had a night of karaoke. So what do Venezuelans sing along to on New Year's eve: rhetorical question of the year, Colombian chucu chucu, what else?2


For our next three days we went to Rancho Grande, a cloud forest reserve around the hotel dreamt by the Ogre of the Andes, Juan Vicente Gómez. This reserve features some of the largest tropical trees I have ever seen less than a day's hike from any road, howler monkeys from the crumbling hotels veranda, and the only Venezuelan endemic we saw, a pair of Handsome fruiteaters. After days of sandwiches and spaghetti with tuna we agreed to collect plants on the road down to the beach of Puerto Colombia near colonial Choroní (hoping for some fresh fish we never saw). This proved to be a fateful decision as three-meter waves washed away my glasses in an accident more ludicrous than unexpected. From then onwards I did my people watching on the beach through binoculars.


Sunburnt, happy and myopic we headed back down to the Llanos at Hato Piñero. Piñero is an 80,000-ha ranch where 66% of the land is left wild and hunting has been forbidden since the 1950s. As a result curassows seem as common as hoatzins, there are foxes, oncilla, capybaras, crocodiles, and deer seem to be a pest. The high point of the trip arrived in the form of a semi mummified Natalus [not really, probably a Myotis] found in one of the rooms. As Rebecka remembers this brings out in me a sort of mad laughter that echoes through the rooms. But no New Year's trip is complete without the Colombian moment, and this would be in Piñero when I realized the owners would not post coordinates or directions on the internet for fear of Colombian guerrillas and their hostage-taking policies.


So Colombia has exported its fears as well. And more than that, it would seem as if Colombia and Venezuela existed as each other's half, each other's alter ego, the latter a country of what we call costeños and llaneros as a response to our Andean heartland. A place where the Andes are still wild (in places at least) and wealth spills and overflows out of the earth rather than from the earth being cleared and plowed and tilled and turned over. A country where caudillos assign themselves limitless powers, in contrast to its neighbor where the most conflicted and longest-running democracy in South America coughs blood with the last viruses of communism financed by the brutal capitalism of the drug market. Alice and I bought postcards at the Caracas airport and could not find stamps or a post office, so I mailed them from Colombia. A fitting memento of our trip, a sign of life from two countries divided by a common culture.


1. Note by Konstantin Läufer: Pastor López is indubitably Venezuelan.

2. Note by Konstantin Läufer: chucu chucu, though inspired by Colombian cumbia, among other rhythms, is in fact... Peruvian!
