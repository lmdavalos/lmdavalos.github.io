After a less than pleasant flight from Frankfurt that made the nine o'clock downtown number nine seem comfortable, I landed in Lisbon. Here I have to clarify that my prior knowledge of Lisbon in particular, and Portugal in general, consisted of an eclectic mix of Lord Byron, Elizabeth Barrett Browning, and a travel channel half-hour special. None of the above had prepared me for almost every passenger lighting up a fag around the luggage carousel upon arrival. These kind souls dispersed themselves among nonsmokers in a perfectly uniform fashion so as to maximise the smoke the rest of us could breath for free.


Soon thereafter I met with Patricia (aka Pat Brito, our Portuguese host), Sim and Lydia. As we drove back from the airport all us Colombia veterans were immediately struck by the architectural and physionomic similarities with the New World: modern Lisbon is as eclectic and contrasting as any in the Latin American continent. Our first impression of Lisbon, the old colonial capital,  was a breathtaking view of the old bairros from the mirador at Anjos. We spotted our prospective touring sites as Pat passionately narrated different episodes in the history of the city, from the Siege to the Age of 'Discoveries,' from the earthquake in the XVIII century to the fire of 1988.


Starting a pattern that would persist throughout the trip, my favourite moment began with an exquisite meal at the so-touristy-you-have to do it Bairro Alto. For those of us who have already spent what seems a lifetime in places like NYC or London, Portuguese service offers a rare opportunity to relax, and spend less money than one had originally planned. Before we ordered anything, several appetizers were laid on the table: sourdough, really good olives, exceptional cheese, thin slices of salami, and farinheira. The real feast consisted of meat cooked in wine, trout stuffed with ham, and salty, garlicky, olive-oily  bacalhau. Sim had the omnipresent vegetarian special: omelette.


The next day after breakfast of fruity buns and what became my staple; sumol de laranja; we set out due East towards the Alentejo via Setúbal with a stop at the Museu de Arroz, home of the best tomato salad I ever had, and then some exquisite cod cakes. We also spotted our first storks, nesting atop the local buildings, and a fair number of waterbirds. We took the long route of backroads through the countryside, while gentle savannas dotted by the occasional shrub (not unlike some parts of the Sabana de Bogotá) gave way to Mediterranean scrub, olive and cork orchards.


In the early evening we crossed the walls of the medieval city of Evora where we quickly set about foraging the local delicacy; chunks of pork  with clams (and we thought we would never see these two things together!) and shark soup. In town we visited the most prominent sites the bimillenial  temple of Diana, the late Gothic Cathedral of Saint Francis, and the Capela dos Ossos.

All three deserve comments: the temple columns stand, as do two of the original beams, the cathedral is a minimalist building whose sober nave contrasts with the rich niches and its frontispiece bearing the pelican, the armillar sphere and the rope of the sailing Portuguese royals. The chapel, finally, is a macabre testament to religiosity at once still nature and desecration. Five-hundred corpses were dug up to create an interior of bones with columns made of skulls, walls of humeri and femurs with a few sections interspersed with splendid azulejos and yet more skulls. Two complete skeletons of adult and child hang from the right front corner.


A dense silence enveloped us as we left Evora (walled city that actually had a permanent Christmas soundtrack playing in the square) for Monsaraz, near the banks of the Guadiana. This ancient town on higher ground will see its environs drown when the walls of the Alqueva Dam rise to block the river and feed the thirsty fields. Palaeolithic excavations and contemporary towns will be relocated. Pat, who has studied the birds of the area for several years considers the benefits paltry, when compared to the disruption this dam will bring to the local ecosystem. Peddling jobs and progress, politicians have told the locals this is the future of the Alentejo.


The future looks like a nameless lake feeding fields of crops too expensive for anyone to buy. With a heavy heart we set on to the Adega of Mourão, where we were lifted by sparagus in scrambled eggs, cozido (chickpeas, pork, sausage, chorizo), manjar dos anjos, and bolo rançoso. The Adega is a local institution and men gather at the front to gobble excellent wines and spontaneously break out in song (as in they sing because they want to, for no one but themselves). A cante jondo from a Spaniard accompanied our meal. The chorus of the song referred to the perennial phobia towards the 'Moors,' while its verses dealt with corrupt priests, priapic youngsters, and busty ladies.

That night, after visiting the local Roman riverine fort, we crossed over to Spain in order to see the hundreds of cranes sleeping by a pond. The melodious chorus of these wailers marvelled us. After roosting at Estremoz we returned to Lisbon for New Year's Eve and this is what we were thinking. While in the city we compulsively visited every sight we were supposed to (you can find this in any tourist guide so I will not expand on it).


The highlight was nearby tourist must-see Sintra, focusing on two widely publicized treasures: the 19th century Palacio da Pena and the 13th century Moorish castle. On foot and under the rain we combined our touristing with the survival skills acquired in years of fieldwork. And we could also afford the luxury of contemptuously smiling when the South African couple that gave us a lift mentioned they had been vacationing in the Algarve. Why use the well-trodden path when one can wind about and reach the Alentejo?
