The devil lives in Veracruz— So much for national security, luggage travels unaccompanied often between Houston and Veracruz, and we learnt this the hard way. Two days of wait in Veracruz [we also got to the Totonac ruin of Cempoala, where we spotted our first cool birds: hooded tanagers, in addition to the ruin, of course] for the not negligible privilege of clipping our nails and a Swiss army knife. Awaiting luggage may be the only reason to loiter in Veracruz during the high season; urban Mexicans crowd the hotels sending prices up, trinkets are sold along the smelly harbor, and there is no refuge from the xylophone players anywhere in the Zócalo. It's hard to fathom what all the tourists went there for —street tacos are superb, but Veracruz is not alone in this— somewhat in the manner that Melgar attracts bogotanos (although Veracruz does have the sea). 

As soon as Cibois's luggage arrived we took a bus to Oaxaca, searching for mole (Liliana), birds (Alice), and toilet seats (Mike). Once a remote city, Oaxaca is a backpacking hub today complete with internet cafes on every corner downtown. There are good reasons to visit, though. Hot chocolate, tamales, balmy weather, affordable hotels, cochinitas pibil, fantastic museums, cafe con leche (Alice's newfound love), and a great ethnobotanical garden with free admission and guided tours. Not having made reservations, we waited for eight hours on the town square befriending a five-year-old son of artisans named Beto.


In Niño Dios country— After being installed in a hotel we proceeded to search for an ecotourism outfit ran in partnership with the local communities of the Pueblos Mancomunados. We also had Mike's shoes repaired at a fraction of what "Andrade shoe repair" of Manhattan had quoted. After two hours through xerophytic plateaus in a second-class chicken bus we arrived at Cuajimoloyas, right at the foot of handsome pine forests. Alice and I proceeded to scout the trails radiating out of the village, finding flowering agaves and avian migrants from the north.


Oedipus Mex— At more than 3000 m elevation, we got winded easily and took a long nap in the afternoon. Our fearless leader (Mike), woke us up so that we could catch the village New Year's festivities. Little did he know that we were about to witness the world's least successful lek. A band of men stood around drinking mezcal and looking generally bored in the town hall. Around 11:30 a truck showed up with four speakers the size of small refrigerators (still no match for Jamaica's) and started blasting heart-wrenching songs about long-lost mothers and how they were the one true love in the face of ungrateful, treacherous women. Really, 11 out of 12 songs were about motherly love, no wonder the women had ran away. We sipped mezcal in styrofoam cups, while Mike insisted on how it all would eventually pick up. It never did and we danced in the street near some bemused children who were lighting fireworks.


The next day we discovered that reasonable people had gone over to the next village when the band failed to show up. Thus we had been in the company of the heartbroken few who were not dragged by their wives to a better place. On New Year's day a Niño Dios procession was the highlight, featuring Three very young Wise  men on horses. This led to one of the most crucial discussions of the trip. Who brings presents? The Niño Dios of course! But how does an newborn infant bring presents? Overweight man in chimney makes sense (at least to Mike), but a child? How about the Easter bunny? How does he lay the chocolate eggs? More intriguingly, how can French adults convince their children that a bell from Rome brings the eggs? Infants are one thing but a bell... That's just bizarre!


Our discussion was tempered, however, by the news that NAFTA's chapter on agricultural products would enter into force with the New Year bringing with it a future of dumped industrial produce from the US. Would the chicken taste like rubber next time we visited? Who knows. Apparently some Mexicans do benefit from NAFTA: ranchers in northern Mexico are more productive than their colleagues in Texas. Still does not mean much in the mountains of Oaxaca...


Misery— We hiked to Llanogrande, another village about 7 km east of Cuajimoloyas. Along the trail we saw numerous endemics, horses and dense pine and oak forest. Management by the local communities left a clear mark: the forests around their villages were standing, despite the constant use of firewood for cooking in villages and cities alike (all street vendors in both Veracruz and Oaxaca relied on firewood). Llano Grande received its name from the featureless plateau were it sits, at the mercy of icy winds. Given that the cabins had no fireplace this was true misery, although the dense forests surrounding the village kind of made up for it. Or not quite, the real treat was the ad libitum food offered by the comedor. We had to leave if we wanted to stop the madness of tortillas, agua fresca, mole, enchipotladas, etc. It was a somewhat cold version of paradise. 

After two nights we hitched a ride back to Oaxaca, an unprecedented feat for the two first world citizens travelling with me. Mike was so thrilled he kept taking pictures from the back of the truck, even as the German woman who jumped in with us threw up repeatedly. Altitude and food got to her or maybe she loathed our rendition on "You are my Sunshine." Back in Oaxaca we visited the Jardín Histórico Etnobotánico, the Museo de las Culturas and Monte Albán, and made plans for our next destination: Xalapa.


Heartbreak (Museo de Antropología closed on Mondays) and stomachache (shaved ice contained god-knows-what, stomach bug ensued and not improved by travelling on bus and two planes) in Xalapa— See? My father was right! Shaved ice is bad for you.[Alice's stream of consciousness] Dávalos doesn't want to work, but we have to write the update to the Mexico guidebook now, before forgetting everything, otherwise people who have high expectations on The Book, like our fearless leader Mike, will again be disappointed and clueless in the wilderness, wandering in touristy towns, asking why, oh why, the museums are not open on Mondays like The Book said, tears dripping endlessly on their cheeks, dragging their feet on unpaved sidewalk for hours, until the locals take pity on them and offer them shelter (sheet-less bed) and food (tacos and shaved ice with condensed milk).
